Member Spotlight

Ioannis Koutroulis, M.D., Ph.D., M.B.A, research focuses on sepsis metabolism and how decreased energy production affects innate immunity. His primary goal is to identify treatment targets that will improve the metabolic profile of septic patients and subsequently alleviate the hyperimmune response that leads to multi-organ dysfunction and septic encephalopathy. He is also working on early-warning sepsis tools that can detect sepsis at the onset of the clinical syndrome in order to initiate treatment for improved outcomes.

Dr. Koutroulis also spends a lot of his time teaching the next generation of physicians. He loves traveling all over the world and his favorite place is Santorini, Greece, where he enjoys the beautiful sunsets.

After a year of travel nursing, Dr. Waldron was hired as a staff RN in the NICU at Children’s National in January 1999. She became the NICU clinical instructor in 2002 affording her the opportunity to work collaboratively with other nurse educators and in 2006 earned her MSN-Ed degree from the University of Phoenix. In 2010, she transitioned to Nursing Education & Professional Development as a professional development specialist, where she led education initiatives in the hospital as an American Nursing Credentialing Center (ANCC) Nurse Planner for continuing professional education with inter-professional groups in addition to facilitation of multiple cohorts in the new graduate RN residency program. Hired in 2014 as the Research Nurse Coordinator for the department of Nursing Science, Professional Practice and Quality, she had the opportunity to actively participate in multiple descriptive and experimental nursing research studies. 

She has also served as a faculty planner, presenter and mentor for the quality improvement fellowship program for clinical nurses at Children’s National and worked on the team that developed and implemented the BearEvidence QI resources. Dr. Waldron received her PhD in Nursing Education from the M. Louise Fitzpatrick College of Nursing at Villanova University in 2019 and her dissertation research focus was exploration of the relationships between parental health literacy and discharge transition readiness in parents of Black preterm infants. Dr. Waldron was promoted to the role of Nurse Scientist and received an appointment as an assistant professor of Pediatrics from the George Washington University School of Medicine & Health Science in 2020.

Dr. Waldron is certified in professional development and has adjunct faculty experience teaching pre-licensure nursing students in both clinical and didactic pediatric courses. Additionally, she has served as a peer evaluator for the Accreditation Commission for Education in Nursing (ACEN) since 2010 evaluating nursing programs alignment with their six categories of accreditation standards. Dr. Waldron’s interests are mixed methods, qualitative, intervention and longitudinal research methods; focused on understanding and ameliorating the disparate health outcomes for minority infants and childbearing families.

Alexandra (“Alex”) Brugler Yonts, MD is an attending physician within the Division of Infectious Diseases at Children’s National Hospital and an Assistant Professor of Pediatrics at The George Washington University School of Medicine and Health Sciences. In addition to her clinical responsibilities as an attending physician on the general and transplant infectious diseases inpatient consult teams, she is also the director of the new multidisciplinary COVID-Longitudinal Care Clinic. Dr. Yonts completed her Pediatric Infectious Diseases training through the combined Children’s National/ US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) fellowship track, with a scholarly focus on regulatory science of vaccines and other biologics. Prior to her time at Children’s National, Dr. Yonts received her medical degree from the University of Nebraska and completed her pediatric residency and a chief resident year at the University of California, Davis. Her current primary scholarly interest focuses on the multidisciplinary care model and patient outcomes in complex, post-infectious conditions, such as Acute Flaccid Myelitis (AFM) and Post-Acute COVID Syndrome (PACS). Additional scholarly interests include clinical research in vaccines and other novel antimicrobial therapies, medical trainee well-being and education and quality improvement as it relates to antimicrobial stewardship. Outside of work, Dr. Yonts enjoys baking (often to the benefit of her co-workers), hiking, music, traveling to visit family and friends and going to sporting events with her husband.
